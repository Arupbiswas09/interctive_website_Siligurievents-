Haldi is the morning that decides the mood of your wedding. If it feels rushed, the next four days feel rushed too. If it feels warm and slow and yellow, the wedding will feel that way too.

We have designed Haldi mornings for families in Siliguri, Sevoke, and tucked-away venues near Bagdogra airport. The things that actually go wrong are never the things the YouTube tutorials warn you about.

## The Haldi (and Gaye Holud) — what's the actual difference?

In Bengali tradition, Gaye Holud is the ritual; "Haldi" is the broader North Indian term most families now use interchangeably. Functionally, they describe the same morning — turmeric paste blessed by elders, applied to the bride and groom, surrounded by close family.

The decor brief is essentially the same. The colour story is yellow, marigold, mango leaves, white drape, and one accent — usually saffron or a softer cream. The seating is informal. The food is light.

What changes between communities is the order in the week and the secondary rituals attached. Bengali families often do separate Gaye Holud ceremonies at the bride's and groom's homes; many North Indian families combine both into one event.

## When in the wedding week does Haldi go?

In Siliguri, the practical sequence we see most often is:

- **Two days before the wedding** — Aashirvaad (blessing) at home, evening.
- **One day before the wedding** — Haldi in the morning, Mehendi in the afternoon, Sangeet at night.
- **Wedding day** — Bibaho in the evening.

Squeezing Haldi onto the wedding day itself is a mistake. The turmeric stain stays on skin for hours; the bride's MUA needs a clean canvas in the late afternoon. Always give Haldi its own morning.

## Decor: marigolds, mango leaves, and the case for restraint

The Haldi morning is doing most of the visual work for you. Turmeric is already a deeply photographed colour. Marigold petals already carry the room. White cotton drape already softens everything.

The mistake most decor briefs make is adding more — a third floral colour, a sequinned backdrop, a metallic prop wall. They flatten the photographs.

Our default brief for [Haldi decoration in Siliguri](/services/haldi-gaye-holud) is three things: a marigold-and-mango-leaf swag, an ivory cotton drape that catches morning light, and a low seating arrangement with woven cane mats. We add one floral pillar near the bride's chair only if the venue is large enough to absorb it.

## The Haldi-stain problem — how we protect the venue and your saree

This is the unglamorous part of the brief, and it's the part that distinguishes a decorator who has done Haldis from one who hasn't.

Turmeric stains everything: drapes, upholstery, marble floors, light fabrics, your mother's saree. Our standard protections:

1. **Drop cloths** under the seating circle — heavy cotton, never plastic (it slips).
2. **Stain-rated drape fabric** for any backdrop within 2 metres of the bride and groom — we use cotton in muted tones, not silk.
3. **Disposable thalis and ghatis** for the turmeric, not the family's brass.
4. **Two staff** stationed with damp cloths during the application.
5. **Saree advice** to the family — wear something washable; we provide a kurta swap-over option for the bride before lunch.

Hotel ballrooms in Siliguri (Mainak, Sinclairs) have stricter rules about stain risk than independent banquet halls. We brief venue managers in writing the day before.

## Guest count, timing and food: realistic numbers for North Bengal

A North Bengal Haldi is typically 30–60 guests. Above 80, the ritual feel breaks down and it starts becoming a lunch reception.

Timing is morning — 9:30 to 12:30 is the standard window. We arrive on site at 6:00 AM for setup. Catering is light: a thali lunch, lemon water, some fruit, sweets. No alcohol.

If you are doing a larger Haldi (100+ guests), you need a different venue brief — a covered lawn with shade, not a banquet hall — and a planner who can manage flow.

## The questions to ask a decorator before you book

A short list, in order:

1. How many Haldi ceremonies have you done in this venue?
2. What's your standard stain-protection setup?
3. Can you show me a photograph of a Haldi morning lit naturally — no flash, no fill?
4. What's the lead time you need for floral order?
5. Are you the team that will be on site that morning, or a different crew?

The last question is the one most families forget to ask. It is the one that matters most.
