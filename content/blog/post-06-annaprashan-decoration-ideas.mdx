Annaprashan is small. It is meant to be — six to eight months old, the baby is barely paying attention to the ceremony, and the family is there to eat together and to put the first grain of rice in a tiny mouth.

We design these often, mostly in Siliguri homes and small banquet halls. The families that come away happiest are the ones that did not try to make the day look like a wedding.

## Annaprashan — what's happening and who's there

Annaprashan (অন্নপ্রাশন; "rice initiation") is the Bengali ritual marking a baby's first taste of solid food. The ceremony is typically held at six or seven months for boys, five or six for girls — though families now decide based on the baby's readiness, not strict calendrical rules.

The core elements are: an elder feeds the baby a spoonful of payesh or rice from a silver thali; the baby is then presented with a set of symbolic objects (a book, a pen, a coin, an earthen pot, rice grains, jewellery, and a small idol) and chooses one — a playful augury of vocation.

Attendees are typically immediate family, grandparents, godparents, and a small extended circle. The mood is intimate.

## Scale: a 30-guest home, a 60-guest hall, a 100-guest weekend

The three formats we design most often:

1. **30-guest home ceremony** — Annaprashan at the family's flat or house in Siliguri. Decor is restrained: a small puja corner, a thali table, a chair for the baby, light florals. Catering is a sit-down lunch.
2. **60-guest banquet hall** — usually a small hall on Sevoke Road or near the family's neighbourhood. Decor adds a low backdrop for photographs, dining table florals, and a welcome arrangement. Catering is buffet-style.
3. **100-guest weekend lunch** — held at a mid-size hall or hotel restaurant. Often combined with [Naamkaran](/services/naamkaran) or Mukhebhaat for one afternoon. Decor scales up to include a dedicated photo backdrop and a small stage for the baby's chair.

Above 100 guests, you are functionally hosting a reception — which is fine, but it changes the ritual feel. We will say so honestly during the brief.

## Decor palette: cream, gold, marigold, and one accent colour

Our default palette for Annaprashan is the same one we use for the most-loved ones we have designed:

- **Cream** — drape, table linen, soft chair covers
- **Gold** — thali, accent ribbon, lantern frames
- **Marigold** — pillar arrangements, dining centrepieces
- **One accent colour** — usually a soft green, sometimes a powder pink, occasionally a deep coral if the family asks

We avoid red. Red signals wedding and other major rituals; on a baby's first solid-food day it reads heavy.

We avoid metallic balloon arches. They have become the default for "baby's first" events on Instagram and they age poorly in photographs.

## The baby's chair, the thali, and the seven items tradition

The visual centre of an Annaprashan is the baby's chair — a small carved wooden chair (often the family's own, or rented) placed on a low platform with a floral backdrop directly behind. The thali sits on a small table beside it.

The seven symbolic objects are arranged on a separate small platform, in a wide arc the baby can reach:

1. A book
2. A pen
3. A coin
4. An earthen pot
5. Rice grains in a small bowl
6. A piece of gold jewellery
7. A small idol

Decor brief for these elements: matte tones, no glitter, photographable from above. The photographer will want a top-down shot of the baby choosing; design for that frame from the start.

## Photographs you'll actually want — the shot list

The shot list we coordinate with the family's photographer:

- The first spoon being offered by the eldest family member
- The baby's reaction (whatever it is — laughter, surprise, mild confusion, occasional tears)
- The arc of seven objects from above
- The baby choosing
- The family group photo around the thali table
- A quiet portrait of the parents with the baby in their lap, near the backdrop

That's the whole shot list. Annaprashan does not need 800 photographs. Sixty really good ones are the goal.

## A quick word on Naamkaran and Mukhebhaat combinations

Many North Bengal families combine Annaprashan with [Naamkaran](/services/naamkaran) (the naming ceremony, traditionally on the 11th day after birth but increasingly held later) or with Mukhebhaat (the maternal grandparents' visit ritual, which often coincides with Annaprashan in Bengali families).

The decor brief for a combined day stays the same — same palette, same scale — but we add:

- A small **naming card** display near the baby's chair (often a hand-lettered card with the chosen name in Bengali and English)
- A **second thali** for the Mukhebhaat sweets

A combined afternoon runs longer (3.5 to 4 hours instead of 2) and lunch is typically more elaborate. Plan accordingly.
