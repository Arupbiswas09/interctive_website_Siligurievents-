A mandap is a small room your wedding happens inside. We try to remember that — because every brief we get for "a viral mandap" eventually settles into a quieter conversation about how the family sits, where the priest needs space, what the camera lens sees from the front row, and whether the canopy survives the second day.

This is a working guide to the design choices that age well.

## What a mandap is actually doing, structurally and emotionally

Structurally, a mandap is four corner posts (or two, or eight), a canopy, and a defined floor. Anything more is decoration on that frame.

Emotionally, it's the room where the marriage actually happens. It's tiny — six to ten people inside it, including the priest and the families. The other 300 guests are watching, but they are watching a small room. Every design decision should serve those ten people first and the watching 300 second.

The mandaps that age well are designed inside out. The shape is decided by who sits where. The canopy is decided by what the priest and bride and groom need to be able to see and do. The florals are decided by what the camera reads from row one and row eight, not what the social-media reference looks like in isolation.

## The four families of mandap shapes — and which suits which venue

We work with four core shapes:

1. **Square** — four posts, square canopy. The most traditional and the most photogenic from any angle. Suits banquet halls with a wide central aisle.
2. **Round** — circular canopy on four or six posts. Soft. Works beautifully in venues with a curved or apsidal front wall. Common in [Bengali wedding](/services/bengali-wedding) briefs.
3. **Octagonal** — eight posts, intricate canopy. Visually maximalist. Needs a venue with a 13-foot-plus ceiling to breathe.
4. **Open / canopy-less** — two posts behind, a floral arch in front, no overhead canopy. Increasingly popular for outdoor venues — tea gardens, lawns, hill resorts.

The mistake we see most often: an octagonal mandap in a venue with a 10.5-foot ceiling. The canopy crushes the space. Always check the ceiling height before agreeing the shape.

## Florals: marigold density, orchids, and the case for green

The default Indian wedding mandap brief is "more flowers." We argue against it almost every time.

Floral density beyond a certain point becomes visually flat. The camera reads it as a wall of colour rather than a composed image. Our default mandap floral brief:

- A **dense floral cap** at the canopy edge — marigold, rose, and one accent (orchid or carnation)
- Floral pillars at the four corners, tapered toward the top
- **Green foliage** between flowers to give the eye places to rest
- A single dramatic floral element behind the priest's space — never both behind and overhead

The case for green is photographic. Greenery makes flowers look more like flowers. A fully floral mandap looks heavy on camera; a mandap with a 30% green element looks alive.

## Light: warm-white, candle, lanterns — never RGB

We are firm on this. RGB strip lighting destroys mandap photography. The camera can't white-balance against a moving colour temperature, and the family's skin tones go magenta or cyan.

The lighting brief for a mandap is:

- **2700–3000K warm-white spots** focused on the bride, groom and priest
- **Candle clusters** at the canopy posts (LED candles if the venue restricts flame)
- **Lantern lines** if the mandap is in an outdoor or transitional space
- A **soft uplight** on the floral cap to give the canopy edge dimension at night

That's the whole brief. No DJ-style colour wash, no strobe, no neon.

## Backdrops vs canopies — the trade-off most couples miss

The trade-off: a strong backdrop makes the mandap photograph well in landscape (the photographer's pulled-back shot). A strong canopy makes it photograph well in portrait (the close-up of the bride and groom).

Most mandaps cannot afford to do both at maximum intensity — the result is visually noisy. Pick one. We push couples toward a strong canopy and a quiet backdrop, because the closer shots are the ones the family prints.

For an outdoor venue, the answer is usually "no backdrop at all" — the landscape is the backdrop. See our [tea-garden wedding guide](/blog/tea-garden-wedding-north-bengal-guide) for more.
