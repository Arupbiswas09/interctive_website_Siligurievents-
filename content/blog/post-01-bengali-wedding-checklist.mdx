A Bengali wedding in North Bengal isn't one event. It's an arc — seven mornings, six nights and one quiet Bidaay. We have designed dozens of them across Siliguri, Jalpaiguri and the tea-garden belt, and the pattern that actually works isn't a planner spreadsheet from Pinterest. It's a six-month rhythm that respects the rituals and gives every supplier — decor, MUA, catering, photographer — the lead time they need.

This is what we hand over to families we work with.

## Why a 6-month runway works for a Bengali wedding

Three months is what most North Bengal families allow themselves. It is also why most of them end up paying surge rates for venue, decor and MUA in November, December and January.

Six months gives you three things: a venue you actually chose (not "the one that was free"), a decor brief that was thought through rather than copy-pasted, and breathing room when something goes wrong — and something always goes wrong, usually two weeks before the date.

If you have more than six months, you do not start earlier. You let the first month sit. The reason is human: families need time to agree on the date, the city, the venue category and the guest-list scale. Skipping that conversation creates the friction that surfaces at month three.

## Month 6: lock the date, venue, and decorator

By the end of month six you have three things on paper: the wedding date (with all rituals dated against it), the venue, and the decorator. Nothing else.

In Siliguri the popular dates between mid-November and February-end get booked twelve months in advance for hotel ballrooms (Mainak, Sinclairs, Cygnett, Royal Sarovar) and six to eight months for banquet halls along Sevoke Road and Hill Cart Road. Outdoor venues around Salugara and Matigara hold out longer but get expensive in the December peak.

The decorator brief at this stage is simple: ceremony count, guest count, venue, and a budget band. Specific decor ideas come later. We meet at the venue if we can.

## Months 5–4: rituals, guest list, and a Pakka decision on Aiburo Bhaat

These are the conversation months. You finalise:

- Which rituals you are doing — full Bengali arc (Aashirvaad, Aiburo Bhaat, Gaye Holud, Mehendi, Bibaho, Bashar Ghor, Bou Bhaat) or a curated subset.
- Which families host which ritual — Aiburo Bhaat at the bride's home is non-negotiable for most; Bou Bhaat at the groom's is almost always at a different venue.
- The guest list — three lists in a Google Sheet, one per family + one combined.
- The MUA and the photographer — both book out before December.

We send the decorator brief in week one of month four. It includes ritual-by-ritual venue notes, family preferences (vegetarian Aiburo Bhaat, no flowers in the Bashar Ghor, etc.), and a colour direction that's no more than three colours plus white.

## Months 3–2: Gaye Holud, Mehendi and Sangeet — the decor briefs

This is where the real design work happens. Each pre-wedding day gets its own brief — they are not interchangeable.

[Gaye Holud (or Haldi)](/services/haldi-gaye-holud) reads yellow and marigold. The decor problem to solve is stain protection, not visual maximalism — a covered courtyard with ivory drapes is harder to design than it looks.

[Mehendi](/services/mehendi) wants low seating, soft light, and a backdrop the photographer can use for an hour. The seating count is usually 30–60 — much smaller than the wedding.

[Sangeet](/services/sangeet) is the night that asks the most of a decorator. A stage, a dance floor, a sound brief that respects the venue's bylaws (most Siliguri halls cap music at 10 PM), and lighting that flatters every skin tone in your family.

We send concept boards by end of month three. Approvals close by the first week of month two.

## The final month: Bashar Ghor, Saat Paak and Bidaay

Month one is execution, not planning. We rehearse setup with the venue. We confirm the priest's space at the mandap. We agree the chair count for Saat Paak. We ask the family which photographs they want from Bashar Ghor — that one ritual produces some of the most-printed photos of the wedding, and many photographers don't know what to do with it.

This is the month to stop adding ideas. Every late addition is a risk to the schedule and the budget.

## After the wedding: Bou Bhaat, photo edits, and saying thanks

[Bou Bhaat](/services/reception) lives at the groom's end. It is functionally a reception and is often the largest single-evening gathering of the entire wedding.

Photo edits land 4–8 weeks after the wedding. Video edits, longer. We send a written thank-you to the venue and to every supplier we worked with — small thing, and it pays for itself the next time we book.
